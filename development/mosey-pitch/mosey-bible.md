# 1. Overview & Thesis

## 1.1 What Mosey is

**A little dog who loves you and can't wait to go outside with you.**

Mosey is a walking companion app. Not a fitness tracker, not a step counter, not a coach. You share your life with a small hand-drawn dog who lives on your phone, adores you without condition, and would like — whenever you're ready, no pressure, truly — to go see what's out there.

The app's world is your real neighborhood, lightly enchanted: a mailbox at slipper-distance with a little red flag, a bench worth sitting on, a tree that keeps the seasons, a pigeon with a job nobody gave it. Walking to these places is the game. There is no losing.

**The strategy sentence:** Finch × Hot Girl Walk — the self-care pet, but the self-care is going outside.

**The heart of it.** Mosey is about the small things: a good stick, the smell before rain, four minutes outside on a gray day. Little moments, felt bigger than they have any right to be. The emotional promise is that nothing has to *happen* for a day to be worth something — and Mosey embodies that by feeling enormous joy about nothing in particular. The signature move of the whole product is the juxtaposition: a flat, deadpan observation ("There was no occasion to celebrate today.") over Mosey doing a little dance in a party hat. The narrator states the ordinary; Mosey finds it larger than life. That gap — small things, larger-than-life feelings — is the reason the app exists.

## 1.2 The name

*Mosey* (v.): to walk in an unhurried way, without a fixed destination.

The thesis is in the dictionary definition: the anti-Strava, in one word. It near-rhymes with *cozy*, is gender-ambiguous, and verbs naturally — users will "go for a mosey" unprompted. It is also the character's default name; users rename freely. The title pairs the name with an explainer that carries ASO weight: **Mosey: A Walking Companion** (working).

*Open question — the Mozy respelling (mirrors "cozy" visually; more distinctive; but surrenders the dictionary meaning, signposts the cozy link, and reads startup rather than storybook). Both spellings have software-trademark collisions (Mozy: the defunct EMC backup brand; Mosey: a live compliance startup at mosey.com) — a proper search is required either way. Current recommendation: Mosey.*

## 1.3 The thesis: the door is the hardest step

The enemy is not laziness; it is activation energy. The decision to walk, made on the couch, is expensive. The same decision, made standing on the sidewalk, is nearly free. Almost every fitness product places its entire ask on the wrong side of the door ("Ready for your 20-minute walk?" is a couch-priced question) and then punishes the user for declining.

Mosey's compassion is architectural, not copywritten:

- **The app never asks for a walk.** It only ever offers the mailbox — a concrete, bounded, ninety-second curiosity with a guaranteed payoff.
- **Longer walks are never committed to indoors.** They are discovered outside, through optional invitations, where the decision is cheap and self-authored.
- **The mailbox walk is complete in itself. Always.** It is never a lobby for the "real" content.
- **The app has no memory of your noes.** Declined invitations are not stored — not logged, not counted, not referenced. Only yeses are data. The app is structurally incapable of guilt.

## 1.4 How it works — the loop in one paragraph

A red flag on a hand-drawn mailbox is the app's only summons. Flag up: something came — a letter, a field report, an object, a seed. You walk to it, at slipper-distance calibrated to your range. Sometimes Mosey's ears go up at something down the street: an invitation, yours to take or leave, unrecorded either way. Walks yield finds; finds can be kept, displayed, or sent to the world's residents, who reply later, disproportionately, raising the flag on some future morning. When a walk reaches somewhere, Mosey offers to keep it — the camera opens, Mosey stands in your real photo, and the moment files into the journal as a keepsake. The world reveals itself at the pace you walk. At home, opening the app is never a menu but a window: Mosey is always mid-something, and you walk in on it.

The loop, made legible: **flag up → walk to it → find something → (sometimes) keep the moment → the journal remembers.** Everything else — invitations, sends, dreams, residents — hangs off that spine.

Retention runs on curiosity and continuity, never obligation: mail with momentum, a world keyed to real weather and seasons, objects that get remembered, a character worth checking on. The reward for opening the app is never a number. It's finding out what happened.

## 1.5 What Mosey is not

Each rejected lane has a body attached (full market analysis in §2):

- **Not a walking game.** Non-IP walking games (Walkr, Wokamon) are a proven revenue dead end; the IP-backed exceptions (Pikmin Bloom, Pokémon Sleep) aren't reachable comps.
- **Not a fitness tracker with personality.** Gentler Streak owns kind-tracking, with Apple's awards, and it's still a modest business.
- **Not step-rewards.** Sweatcoin's sentiment collapse is the permanent warning: money touching the movement loop destroys trust.
- **Not an AI companion.** 337+ of those, churning 30% faster than non-AI apps. Mosey is authored, silent, and gets you off the couch — the counterpoint to a lane blamed for keeping people on it.
- **Not clinical mental health.** No claims, no therapy-speak, ever. Support, not fixing.

Category: Health & Fitness (where the audience searches and pays). Language: zero fitness vocabulary anywhere. Business model: the Finch model — subscription + expression economy (wardrobe, cosmetics); the relationship is monetized through love, never through need, and never through the walk itself.

## 1.6 The doctrines

Bright-line rules. Each settles a future debate.

1. **The app never shows a number that hasn't happened yet.** No targets, no goals, no "2km to go." Past-tense numbers only, small, in the journal. Warmth hierarchy: places > time together > distance > *never* steps, calories, or pace.
2. **The app has no memory of your noes.** Declines are not data. Architecturally.
3. **Every feature is a resident.** Anything a wellness app would put in a dashboard, Mosey embodies in the world: notifications → the flag; goals → destinations; the streak → the Tortoise's cumulative journey; weather excuses → the Puddle; rest → the Bench; self-compassion → the Snail. If a feature can't be embodied, it probably doesn't belong.
4. **No streaks. Nothing can be broken.** Track "days together" and other up-only numbers. The return after absence is the most-designed moment in the app, and it is joyful.
5. **Complete in itself.** Every outing, at every length, is a whole story. The mailbox walk, the bench sit, the four-minute loop — all filed as full accomplishments, because they are.
6. **Nothing is single-use.** Generated content earns meaning through memory: finds, answers, and events resurface later, transformed. Continuity is where meaning lives.
7. **The app stays in character.** It never drops the fiction to explain itself, point out its own jokes, or reassure you it's "just an app." The narrator commits; the world is treated as real. (The Calvin & Hobbes rule — Hobbes is never confirmed as a stuffed toy.)
8. **Routes are never stored.** Anchors are a handful of user-chosen points; home region is coarse; processing is on-device where possible. Privacy is a brand feature, stated plainly.

## 1.7 The voice, in four frames

Canonical marketing copy — this is the tone reference for everything:

> *[Mosey looking out the window]*
> There's a whole world out there to explore.
>
> *[the red mailbox, flag up]*
> ...but some days you only make it to the mailbox.
>
> *[Mosey curled up, asleep]*
> or maybe not even that.
>
> **Mosey. A walking companion. [pause] who understands <3**

*(If the Mozy respelling is adopted, the name swaps in place. The final line performs the afterthought mechanic — the tagline demonstrates the voice. The `<3` is typeable ASCII, not emoji: the typewriter could type it. See rule 28.)*

Style rules it demonstrates: short declaratives · no exclamation marks · compassion in the structure, never stated · undersell every payoff · the user is always on the inside of the joke.

### House style — the writing rules

Enforceable rules for every word in the app, marketing, and store copy. When in doubt, cut.

**Sentences & grammar**

1. Short declarative sentences. One idea each. If a sentence carries two feelings, split it.
2. The narrator reports live in present tense. Field reports and journal entries are past tense.
3. No exclamation marks, ever. Excitement lives in the content, never the punctuation. Question marks are rare — the narrator seldom asks.
4. When a line jokes, the punchline lands in the final clause and nothing follows it. But not every line jokes — some lines are just true — and the shapes must rotate (plain observation, afterthought, strikethrough, escalating facts). If five consecutive lines share a rhythm, break one. A punchline earns its place only if it reveals something true from an unexpected angle; if it merely adds attitude, end on the fact instead.
5. Understatement is the default intensity. The bigger the moment, the flatter the delivery.
6. Periods do the work. Em dashes sparingly; ellipses only to signal the narrator's reluctance to continue.
7. Surface lines run one to two sentences. Only field reports and letters may run longer.

**The dog on the page**

8. Mosey is always "Mosey" — never he, she, they, it, or "the dog." The repetition is part of the voice. (In-app, renders as the user's chosen name.)
9. Mosey never speaks directly. Mosey's words arrive only as reported speech, quoted inside the narrator's sentences: *Mosey says the shelf "wasn't working."* Translation reliability stays ambiguous.
10. Specificity over category, always: "the third-shiniest button," never "an item." Numbers appear only as comedy precision ("Day 9. Four meters.") or past-tense fact.

**Addressing the user**

11. "You" appears sparingly and only factually ("You went outside for four minutes."). The app never commands — no imperatives, no "go," no "try," no "don't forget."
12. The narrator never evaluates the user. No praise words ("great job," "proud of you") — instead, facts that imply ("He's telling everyone.").
13. The user is never the butt of a joke. The dog, the Pigeon, and the narrator's own composure are the acceptable casualties.

**Banned vocabulary**

14. Fitness-speak: workout, exercise, calories, steps, pace, goal, streak, challenge, crush, progress.
15. Wellness/therapy-speak: journey, mindful, wellness, self-care (in-app; permitted in ASO metadata only), gratitude, healing, you've got this.
16. App-speak: unlock, level up, achievement, reward, XP, bonus, limited time, don't miss out, last chance.
17. Corporate empathy: "we understand," "you deserve," "we're here for you." Compassion is structural, never stated.

**Comedy shapes** (a menu, not a sequence)

18. Escalating flat facts: "Mosey heard you pick up your keys. Mosey is already at the door. Mosey has been at the door for some time."
19. Register clash: solemn vocabulary applied to tiny subjects. Officialese for the squirrel war ("NOTICE OF INTENT"), logistics for the Snail ("the shipment is thriving"), meteorology for Mosey ("Forecast:").
20. The declined investigation: "No explanation was offered." "The narrator has questions." "No further information is available."
21. **The afterthought** — the typewriter can't delete, only continue. Direct statement, `[beat]`, then an addition that reconsiders the thought: "Mosey is fine. [beat] Mosey has been at the door for an hour." Roughly one line in four.
22. **The strikethrough** — the narrator types, pauses, and crosses out the part that overreached (the strike stays visible). Strike and stop, or strike and replace inline — **never restate the whole sentence.** Reading it with the struck words skipped must leave the truer line: "Mosey woke up ~~happy to start the day~~." (leaves "Mosey woke up." — no repeat). Can interact with animation (claim → Mosey contradicts → strike): "Mosey woke up ~~happy to start the day~~. [Mosey: sleepy glare]". One in fifteen or rarer. Sincerity variant strikes *toward* truth: "Mosey missed ~~the walks~~ you."
23. Plain truth, no joke: "You went outside for four minutes. It counted." Roughly half of all lines.
24. Callbacks beat new jokes. Reuse canon: the incident, the wings everyone can see, the hedge.
25. Never explain a joke, never label a feeling, never signpost a punchline. Deadpan does not wink. Never zany — every absurd detail must be behaviorally true of a dog, a pigeon, a snail; the comedy is recognition, not invention.

**Sincerity passages** (3–4 per year)

26. No jokes within a screen of a sincere line. Sentences get shorter, not longer. Concrete images only — sincerity is also factual ("the door opens and it's you"). The banned praise words stay banned even here.

**Formatting & naming**

27. Residents are proper nouns and have no personal names: the Pigeon, the Tortoise, the Cat. Only Mosey is named. Landmark descriptors are few and plain (the Good Bench — alt candidate: the Old Bench); the honorific never spreads to new objects (no "good windowsill").
28. Sentence case everywhere. No title case in UI. No emoji anywhere in the product voice. Typeable ASCII emoticons (`<3`) are permitted — the typewriter could type them — but rationed like sincerity: the tagline spends one; in-app they appear about as often as the sincere letters.

## 1.8 Why now (2026)

- **The audience is assembled.** The Finch/Duolingo cohort — streak-fatigued, wellness-literate, allergic to hustle culture — is now the primary "fitness" app demographic. Walking-as-mood-regulation (hot girl walks, "silly little walks," romanticize-your-life) is a durable mainstream practice, not a trend.
- **Adult-cute is the mass market.** The trinket wave (Jellycat, Sonny Angel, Smiski, Labubu) made cute a mainstream adult identity language. Committing to cute no longer narrows the audience; hedging it does.
- **The white space is real and closing.** Emotional-companion apps (Finch ~$30M ARR, Tolan $12M ARR) and walking games both thrive; nobody has fused an emotionally reactive character with going outside. Widgetable has a GPS pet-walk *feature* with no soul; Finch could ship "take your birb outside" any quarter. Speed matters.
- **The AI-companion flood is the foil.** As screen-bound AI companions multiply (and churn), "the companion that gets you outside" is a fresh position and a ready-made press story.
- **The moat is craft.** Reactive, hand-made character animation with real comic timing cannot be cloned cheaply or quickly. The character is the product; the animation is the character.

# 2. Who It's For

## 2.1 The person

She is 24–40, with adult money and adolescent-grade stress. She knows Jellycat and Sonny Angel by name. She had a Finch bird once and still feels warmly about it. She has Duolingo guilt measured in hundreds of days. She already knows a walk fixes her mood — and still doesn't go. She calls it a "silly little walk" because irony is how her generation protects sincerity.

The load-bearing fact: **the walk is her only unclaimed time.** A job, a partner, kids, roommates, a group chat — everyone needs something from her. This is why Mosey must be company and never a dependent (§3): a companion who needs her is just more of her day. Mosey wants nothing. Mosey just wants to come along.

She is "she" here because the core demographic skews female, but the design is ungendered — the register we've chosen (Peanuts, Gromit, Moomin) reads all-ages and all-genders. Marketing may skew; the product does not.

## 2.2 The feel-seen doctrine

What the app understands about her, and where each understanding lives in the product:

- **Romanticize-your-life is her operating system.** She wants evidence that her ordinary days matter. The journal, the finds, the field reports are exactly that: proof the Tuesday walk was an event. The app is a machine for noticing.
- **The Snoopy mechanism.** A character who feels enormous things about tiny things — a leaf, a stick, the door opening — grants her permission to feel them too. Mosey's unembarrassed delight is the product's core emotional service.
- **Bad days get facts, not therapy.** "You made it to the mailbox" is being seen. "Great job prioritizing your wellness journey" is an HR email. The four-minute walk is filed complete, because it was.
- **Weather is a mood, not an obstacle.** She has feelings about rain and first cold days. So does Mosey. The Puddle exists so that rain is a reason, not an excuse.
- **Rest counts.** The Bench sit is a full outing. The Spider proves staying home is also a life. Nothing in the world models hustle.
- **She can disappear.** Work explodes, a depressive stretch, a vacation — she's gone three weeks. The return is joyful, unexplained, uninterrogated. The Cat models it; the architecture (no memory of noes, nothing breakable) guarantees it.
- **One absolute ban:** body, weight, or calorie language — anywhere, once — ends the relationship. There is no recovering from it with this audience.

## 2.3 How wide is the audience

Wider than "cozy niche." The trinket wave (Labubu on celebrity bags, Jellycat as decor, Sonny Angels on office desks) made adult-cute a mainstream identity language — committing to cute no longer narrows the market; hedging it does. Secondary audiences that fall out of the design for free:

- **Dog owners** — a large organic-acquisition channel (dog people share dog content). Requires one explicit accommodation eventually: real-dog mode, where Mosey and the real dog walk together rather than compete.
- **Body-doubling users** (ADHD, anxiety) — sit-with-me presence and the no-demand structure serve them without a single clinical word.
- **Low-mobility and housebound users** — distance rings scale silently to any range; the Mailbox can be the front step. Same world, same story, every body.

## 2.4 The competitive frame

Five lanes, sorted by what actually pays:

| Lane | Evidence | Lesson |
|---|---|---|
| Emotional companion | Finch ~$30M ARR, bootstrapped; Tolan $12M ARR in months | The money lane. Relationship monetizes — through expression, never need |
| Walking games | Pikmin Bloom $34.8M/2025, Pokémon Sleep $150M lifetime — both IP; Walkr/Wokamon negligible after a decade | IP-only lane. Take its mechanics, never its business model |
| Gentle fitness tracking | Gentler Streak: Apple Watch App of the Year + Design Award, still modest | Awards are a channel, not a business |
| Narrative audio | Zombies, Run!: ~50K subs at peak, declined, bought back by its creator | Authored-content treadmills eat small teams |
| Extrinsic rewards | Sweatcoin: huge installs, review sentiment collapse | Money touching the movement loop destroys trust |

Cautionary tales with names: **Peridot** (world-class charm, no purpose — shut down 2026: the pet must always be a vehicle, never the product). **Aloe Bud** (1.5M downloads, beloved, gentle, ~no business: gentleness without a daily loop and an expression economy earns nothing — "the gentleness tax"). **Kinder World** (no-fail design, ethical monetization, modest results: same tax, VC edition).

Live threats: **Finch** could ship an outside mode any quarter (2M DAU head start). **Widgetable** already has a GPS pet-walk feature (~1M downloads/month) — soulless today, instructive tomorrow. **The AI-companion flood** (337+ apps, 128 launched in 2025) is a foil, not a threat — they churn 30% faster than non-AI apps and stay indoors — but they are raising the bar for "the character knows me." Speed and craft are the answers to all three.

## 2.5 Market evidence, compressed

- 94% of Gen Z report regular mental-health struggles; 80% prioritize small daily joys (little-treat culture). Walking-as-mood-regulation has held for five+ years — a practice, not a trend.
- The performance lane is saturated and not ours: Strava at ~195M users and ~$500M revenue proves the outdoor boom while foreclosing the competitive route.
- Health & Fitness subscription benchmarks: 2–5% of downloads ever pay is the realistic planning band; the category skews annual plans; fitness churns steepest of any category (the January cliff) while companion apps retain — which is the entire argument for §1's positioning.
- Year-one revenue scenarios (full model in §8): ~$45K quiet / ~$400K solid / $2.5M+ breakout. Finch is the ceiling; Aloe Bud is the floor; the difference between them is a daily loop plus an expression economy.

## 2.6 Japan and East Asia

Pokémon Sleep earned $73M of its first $150M in Japan (US: $15M). Character-driven wellness over-indexes in Japan, Taiwan, and Korea, and a dog character localizes with near-zero cultural loss. Japanese, Traditional Chinese, and Korean belong in year one — the cheapest growth lever of this size available. (Production flag from §3: handwriting typefaces for CJK need early planning.)

# 3. The Character Bible

## 3.1 Mosey

One dog, forever. No breeds, no color variants; at most one adoption-time mark (a patch, a spot — one degree of freedom). Users rename Mosey freely; the brand keeps the name in public. Personalization comes from history, not configuration: wardrobe, learned quirks, accumulated finds, a home that fills with shared evidence.

**Essence.** Completely sincere, never ironic. Eager slightly past dignity. In love with outside. Emotionally transparent — Mosey has never successfully hidden a feeling.

**The inner life (the Snoopy correction).** Mosey is not a butler. Mosey has projects, delusions, and feuds: the novel (word count: eleven), the sock interrogations, the squirrel war, a strained professional relationship with the Pigeon. Ratio: for every one thing Mosey does for you, Mosey is caught doing three inexplicable things for Mosey alone. Devotion from a character with a full life feels earned; from a character with nothing else, programmed. Grand gestures (the one-day-anniversary candlelight dinner) land because they interrupt Mosey's nonsense, not because they're the job.

**Company, never a dependent.** Mosey wants the walk, never needs it. Mosey is never hungry, sick, sad-because-of-you, or waiting with a bar draining. The user's world is full of things that need her; Mosey is the one who just wants to come along (§2.1).

**Flaws are canon** — flaws are what make cute read adult: afraid of one particular mailbox (not yours); vain about finds; has never once caught the Squirrel and has no plan; strong unresolved feelings about the vacuum. Flaws never resolve. They are running bits, not arcs.

**Learned quirks.** Mosey drifts toward the user's life: the night-walker's Mosey becomes moon-obsessed; the rain-walker's Mosey develops proud raincoat energy; the park-walker's Mosey holds strong duck opinions. Small, slow, additive — every user's Mosey becomes slightly theirs.

**Philosophy rule.** Mosey may only philosophize about things Mosey can smell, see, or eat. Puddles, wind, the moon — yes. Journeys, growth, life — never. Profundity is only ever an accident, and the narrator declines to dwell on it.

**Mosey is the weather report.** Every morning Mosey has already checked; posture is the forecast, captioned by the narrator ("Forecast: Mosey is lying in the one sunbeam. More sunbeams expected."). The widget is weather-reactive Mosey plus one line — the daily weather glance, colonized.

**The acting spec.** Silent, not mute: sighs, huffs, tail thumps, one distant 2am bark the narrator refuses to explain — never words. The animation is the personality; writing reports it, acting delivers it (the Gromit model: brow and posture carry the soul). Rig priority: ears > posture and weight > tail > brow > everything else. Every emotion must survive the doodle test — readable at widget size, in four values, in half a second.

## 3.2 The narrator contract

- The narrator is never named or explained.
- Dry, precise, matter-of-fact. The narrator carries all irony; Mosey carries all sincerity. The line never blurs.
- **Reported speech** is the only dialogue: Mosey says the shelf "wasn't working." Whether Mosey said any such thing is permanently ambiguous — doctrine 7 applies.
- The narrator observes the user but never evaluates her: no praise, no verdicts, facts that imply. It cannot reference what she didn't do — declines are not data (doctrine 2).
- The narrator is fallible in one direction: it editorializes, catches itself, and corrects the record in public (the strikethrough). Its composure is a legitimate casualty of jokes; the user never is.

## 3.3 The sincerity economy

Irony is the shield that lets this audience accept sincerity; rationed sincerity is the payload.

- 3–4 full sincerity moments per user-year: the anniversary letter, the return after long absence, one or two unannounced.
- Delivery favors objects over interface: Mosey's letters arrive by mail, impossibly (postage: a leaf).
- Within a screen of a sincere line: no jokes, shorter sentences, concrete images only, praise words still banned. "Mosey doesn't know what a year is. Mosey knows the door opens and it's you."
- The strikethrough's sincerity variant — the strike moves *toward* truth: "Mosey missed ~~the walks~~ you." The most expensive line-shape in the app; spend accordingly.

## 3.4 The text system

The unifying fiction: the whole app is a document being written. Ink on cream; the interface is the journal.

**The narrator types.** Monospace — iA Writer Mono (working choice; iA Writer Duo as the warmer duospace option — both open source under the SIL Open Font License, free for commercial use; Courier Prime as alternative). Character-timed reveal with authored beats (`[beat]` ≈ 800–1200ms; pauses at punctuation; a held beat before final clauses on comedy lines). Timing is written, not uniform — the pause is the punchline's engine. No blinking cursor, ever: streaming text behind a cursor is chatbot-coded, the one association this product cannot afford.

**The typewriter cannot delete.** Two signature moves fall out of the medium:

- **The afterthought** — statement, beat, an addition that reconsiders. The first thought stays on the record. (~1 in 4 lines.)
- **The strikethrough** — typed, paused, the overreaching part crossed out with the strike left visible; what remains (or an inline replacement) is the truer line. Never re-type the sentence minus the struck words — the strike already did it. Can play against animation: the narrator claims, Mosey visibly contradicts, the narrator strikes the claim. (~1 in 15 or rarer.)

Both exist because they make the narrator vulnerable: thoughts evolve live, in front of the reader.

**Letters arrive whole.** A narrator line is a performance; a letter is an object — made in the past, read at the reader's pace. Each correspondent has a hand: the Tortoise's slow loops, the Squirrel's furious small caps and underlines, the Crow's flourishes, Mosey's rare shaky print (no misspellings — the hand is the font's job, never the spelling's). The Pigeon does not write; the Pigeon stamps ("DELIVERED WITH DISTINCTION").

**Interaction rules.** Tap always completes a reveal instantly; reduced-motion renders whole with a fade; typewriter audio is a soft low thock, mutable; the bell is diegetic mailbox sound only.

**Production flags.** CJK handwriting faces need early planning (§2.6). The timed reveal cannot be screenshotted — it must be screen-recorded, which makes every in-app moment a ready-made vertical video with built-in comic timing. The presentation layer is also the TikTok format.

## 3.5 The voice

The rules live in §1.7 (house style, 28 rules) and in the `mosey-voice` skill, which encodes all three registers for any writer or AI session. The canonical line library lives in Appendix A and grows there. Register map:

| Register | Where | Reveal | Carrier |
|---|---|---|---|
| Narrator | In-app live text, widget, marketing | Typed, timed | iA Writer Mono |
| Letters | Mail from residents | Whole, at once | Per-resident hands |
| Stamps | The Pigeon | Whole | Rubber stamp |
| Document prose | Bible, decks, store scaffolding | — | Quiet editorial |

Canonical tagline: **"Mosey. A walking companion. [pause] who understands <3"** — the afterthought mechanic performed as brand copy.

## 3.6 The tonal compass

Four fixed points plus a presentation reference. Each contributes one thing; take only that thing.

- **Peanuts** — deadpan melancholy; the dignity of small failures; a character allowed stillness and sadness. (Not: the ensemble size.)
- **Calvin & Hobbes** — wonder in motion; accidental wisdom found while thinking about little things; the dual reality never settled. (Not: Calvin's mania.)
- **Gromit** — the silent acting model; brow, posture, patience. (Not: plots.)
- **Moomin** — cozy philosophical calm; nature as the setting for feeling; melancholy as comfort. (Not: the mythology.)
- **Wes Anderson** (presentation only) — typewriter text, symmetry, deadpan captions over sincere events. (Not: irony-forward detachment.)

The register in one sentence: sincere character, dry narrator, warm world, nobody winks.

## 3.7 Visual identity

- **Not kawaii.** Kawaii is the saturated default of every widget pet and Finch clone, and it reads childish — the one first-impression risk this brand cannot take. The lineage is comic-strip modernist: Peanuts, Miffy, Moomin — flat, line-driven, generous negative space.
- **Mosey's build (working reference):** a hand-drawn, Snoopy-adjacent puppy — a big rounded white head, two large solid-black floppy ears framing the face, two dot eyes, one big oval nose, a small sitting body, and a single curly-squiggle tail. Charming, slightly rough ink line (not a clean geometric vector). Reads instantly from front, back (ears + curl), and in a window. The party hat is the recurring prop.
- **Four values, total:** warm paper cream; warm near-black ink; one signature accent (the collar and the mailbox flag — a red-orange that becomes the brand color); one ambient world-tone carrying time and weather (golden-hour amber, blue evening, gray drizzle). Mosey is constant; the world breathes.
- **The doodle test is an acceptance criterion.** A distracted person draws Mosey on a napkin in ten seconds, recognizably. Fan-drawability is the distribution mechanism — fan art, tattoos, whiteboards. If a design needs shading to read, simplify until it doesn't.
- **Texture reads adult:** visible linework, paper grain, imperfect edges. Digital-smooth gloss reads corporate-kids.
- **Restraint in celebration:** one perfect tail-wag beats confetti. Melancholy is allowed; stillness is allowed. Kids' apps can't hold still — holding still is how this one signals who it's for.
- **Wardrobe never breaks the silhouette.** Costumes (bandanas, terrible raincoats, the one hat) layer onto the icon without altering it — Snoopy in sunglasses is still Snoopy. This is the expression economy's visual constraint: everything sellable must keep Mosey drawable.
- **The map is an artifact** — hand-drawn, not-to-scale, Hundred Acre Wood endpapers (§4). The Mailbox, accent-colored with the flag up, is the second brand icon and candidate app-icon co-star.

# 4. The World & Its Residents

## 4.1 The map

The map is a picture, not an instrument. Hand-drawn, ink on cream, deliberately not to scale, no north, no blue dot — the reference is the Hundred Acre Wood endpapers: geographically useless, emotionally precise. It starts nearly empty (home, the Mailbox, two or three faded smudges) and fills in as things are discovered. Six months in, it is a dense personal illustration of a relationship — and one of the app's best share artifacts.

Nothing is ever drawn on real cartography. No POI databases, no street maps, no route lines.

## 4.2 Anchors and rings

Anchors are born from behavior, then confirmed. The app notices where she actually pauses — a spot ten minutes out where she tends to stop — and Mosey decides that's the Good Bench; she confirms, or drags it elsewhere. Or she plants one deliberately. Relocation is one gesture, no questions asked ("Mosey has agreed to relocate the operation. Mosey asks no questions.").

Rings are distances, not coordinates, and they scale silently to the user's actual range. For one user the Mailbox ring is 150 meters; for a housebound user it is the front step. The system never says so — the map simply draws itself at her scale. Same world, same story, every body. The calibration standard is slipper-distance (§1.3).

The working rings:

| Ring | Anchor | Walk it names | Cadence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Micro (1–2 min) | The Mailbox | the mailbox walk | Daily |
| Short (~10 min) | The Good Bench | the bench sit | As needed |
| Medium (~20 min) | The Tree | the tree check | Weekly-ish |
| Variable | Wherever the Pigeon is | the pigeon run | Few times a week |
| Ephemeral | The Puddle | puddle day | When it rains |
| Stretch | The Lookout (post-MVP) | the big walk | Rare, an event |

The vocabulary is the goal system: nobody sets a target; she decides which resident to visit.

## 4.3 Navigation

There is none, in the conventional sense. For variable destinations, Mosey is the compass: oriented, leaning, ears committed, glancing back with escalating meaning — a bearing and a distance rendered entirely as acting. Arrival detection is a generous radius; close enough always counts. No map UI on walks — heads up, in the world, which is the product's actual goal.

The journal renders each walk as Mosey's version of the route: a small non-geographic squiggle annotated with what mattered (out → leaf event → the good smell → home). Routes are never stored (doctrine 8), so they can never be shown or leaked.

## 4.4 Resident design axioms

A resident earns its place by holding down a unique cell across four axes:

1. **It names a walk** — owns a ring and gives that size of outing a noun.
2. **It embodies one thing the app helps you do** (doctrine 3). If a feature can't be embodied, it probably doesn't belong.
3. **It runs on a content engine, not a content pipeline** — correspondence templates, weather triggers, state machines, procedural placement; authored vignettes as seasoning.
4. **It has a relationship with Mosey** — the cast generates comedy laterally, so the world feels alive even on days you visit no one.

Naming rule: residents have no personal names — the narrator says "the Pigeon," "the Cat." Only Mosey is named. Landmark descriptors are few and plain (the Good Bench; alt candidate: the Old Bench), and the honorific never spreads to new objects.

## 4.5 The cast

**The Mailbox (and the Spider).** The threshold thesis in an object: a bounded ninety-second curiosity with a guaranteed payoff, and the app's only summons (the flag). Two-way — outgoing mail exists, and she raises the flag herself to send. The Spider is the Mailbox's permanent tenant: predates her, never leaves, has rich ongoing projects, is thriving. Empty mailbox = Spider vignette, so the guaranteed payoff holds even on nothing days. The Spider is also the app's quiet blessing on staying home — never stated, just magnificent indoors. Never explained. "The spider was here before you. Respect the arrangement."

**The Pigeon.** Earnest-dumb courier: maximum dedication, zero comprehension. Cannot read — addresses are interpreted by vibes. Every delivery is a triumph; packages arrive stamped "DELIVERED WITH DISTINCTION" and have not been delivered. Walks everywhere despite visible wings, on a principle never articulated. The head tilt is its entire cognitive process, performed at length, resolving nothing. With Mosey: strained professional courtesy; the incident is never discussed. Functions: variable-distance walks (misdeliveries), comedy density, and the world-exposition engine — wrong mail leaks lore, including other residents' correspondence. "This letter is not for you. Mosey has already smelled it. You've both read it. You are now complicit."

**The Tortoise.** Slow *with* direction. Correspondence-only — possibly never seen, which is the point: a character known entirely through letters. On an enormously slow journey somewhere; the user's lifetime distance somehow moves it forward (never explained). Holds the app's only cumulative number and reports it personally, in-letter. First letter arrives day 2 and carries the previous-resident hook. The journey arc is authored (about twelve letters a season); everything between is quiet.

**The Snail.** Slow *without* direction, and complete as it is — the patron saint of small progress. Perpetually mid-crossing; destination unclear, including to the Snail. Trouble vignettes are stakes-free and never urgent (nothing involving a snail can be urgent); helping is mentioned, never assigned. Also the world's slowest courier: cargo placed on the shell arrives in weeks, guaranteed, by a logic no one audits — and the cargo tints the Snail's identity for the voyage (a leaf makes it a vessel; the bottle cap made it armored). Never ranked against the Tortoise: the two slownesses are the two valid walks. "The Tortoise asked the Snail where it was headed. The Snail said 'yes.' They left it there."

**The Crow.** The charming villain — and it trades, never steals, because loss aversion is the enemy's weapon. Swaps your third-shiniest button for a suspicious "gift" and a note of magnificent self-regard ("AN UPGRADE. YOU'RE WELCOME. — C"). Hard rules: never touches canon or sentimental items; everything recoverable; no deadlines. Retrieval is negotiation comedy at the nest — a new ring on the map. Arrives only after the shelf earns gossip, so its appearance is itself a milestone: "Your collection has attracted a criminal element. Congratulations." Occasionally its trade is accidentally exactly what a resident needed, which even the narrator finds suspicious.

**The Cat.** Completely serious. Regal, unaffected, silent, with a gravitas that reads faintly sinister. Shows no emotion, ever; the narrator speculates about its thoughts and is not comforted ("The Cat's tail moved once. The narrator has three theories. None are comforting."). Mosey regards the Cat with reverence and some confusion. Functions: normalizes leaving and returning without apology ("The Cat is back. It has been gone since March. It has offered no explanation and accepted a chair."); the world's one purely anti-transactional resident — never brings, never asks, never participates in the mail economy; occasionally simply *is there*, which is its own statement about what showing up is worth. Appears as evidence before it appears as a character: a shape on the fence at dusk.

**The Squirrel.** The nemesis. Communicates exclusively in escalating formal notices ("NOTICE OF INTENT — THIRD NOTICE"); the war is eternal, the original grievance forgotten by all parties. Sightings only in MVP — a shape in a tree is enough. Mosey has never once caught the Squirrel and has no plan.

**The Tree and the Sapling.** The Tree is the ancient one: the world's clock. Seasons play out on it — first blossom, first bare branch, first snow — which makes it the live-ops calendar at near-zero marginal cost. It has tenants: residents take up temporary residency, and Mosey has opinions about the vetting process. Mosey respects the Tree; the narrator stopped asking. The Sapling is the day-2 seed, planted where she chooses: it grows in real weeks and months, the totem of the relationship — and in year three, something small nests in it. The Tree is deep past; the Sapling is growing future.

**The Puddle.** Ephemeral: exists only after rain, hosts exclusive finds, and inverts the number-one walking excuse — rain becomes the only day a location exists. Mosey's puddle philosophy is canon. Flagship of a future family of weather-residents (fog, first frost).

**The Good Bench.** A destination — the point is that she went somewhere to be still. The walk that's really a sit, filed as complete. Home of the reflection layer: the one-question walks and dedications live here (§5). Has regulars — the same jogger, the pigeon congress, a dog whose owner Mosey acknowledges with formal nods — a recurring cast observed and never met, which is the pleasant anonymity of real bench-sitting.

**The previous resident.** The unseen mystery: the Tortoise's unanswered letters, the unexplained key, things found that aren't hers. Fills the long-arc curiosity need precisely by never appearing. Never resolved quickly; possibly never resolved.

## 4.6 The contrast matrix

| Axis | Pair | Tension |
|---|---|---|
| Slow with direction ↔ slow without | Tortoise ↔ Snail | The two valid walks — never ranked |
| Fast & wrong ↔ slow & faithful | Pigeon ↔ Snail | The two couriers |
| Gives ↔ takes | Mosey ↔ Crow | Gift economy vs. trade racket |
| Never leaves ↔ returns unexplained | Spider ↔ Cat | Both fine. That's the point. |
| Earnest ↔ scheming | Mosey ↔ Squirrel | The eternal war |
| Emotional transparency ↔ total opacity | Mosey ↔ Cat | The narrator can read one of them |
| Present ↔ absent | Everyone ↔ the previous resident | The mystery under the world |

The matrix encodes the thesis: nearly every axis is a pair of equally legitimate ways to live — fast/slow, out/home, purposeful/aimless, leaving/staying. Nobody in this world is doing it wrong.

## 4.7 Tiers and foreshadowing

Two tiers of presence keep the world feeling populous while animating almost nothing beyond Mosey:

- **MEET (animated, present):** Mosey, the Spider, the Pigeon, Squirrel sightings. Three and a half animated characters at launch.
- **HEAR ABOUT (mentions, mail, evidence):** the Tortoise (letters only), the Crow (rumors, a glint in a tree), the Cat (a shape on the fence), the Snail, the previous resident, the Bench regulars. Depth through hearsay costs one line of copy; depth through meetings costs animation.

Foreshadowing is a formal system: every future resident is seeded one to two weeks before appearing — largely via Pigeon misdelivery, whose errors are the exposition engine. Updates land as the world keeping a promise, not as patch notes. Staging calendar lives in §8.

# 5. Emotional Hooks & Narrative Systems

The design principle under everything here: the reward for opening the app is never a number — it's finding out what happened. Streaks and badges make users calculate; unresolved narrative, memory, and unearned gifts make users wonder. Wondering can't be broken by a missed day, which is why this entire architecture is natively non-judgmental.

## 5.1 Home, and the Peek

Home is one room. It is the same room every time — the place Mosey lives and the place you come back to after every walk. It has three things, and each one means something:

- **A window.** Mosey goes to it to look out, and when he does, he faces away from you — watching. The window shows the weather and the season: rain on the glass, first snow, low golden light. It is also where memory lives — sometimes an old keepsake photo (§5.10) rests in the window, and Mosey stands at it looking out, as if remembering that walk. Weather, world, and memory in one frame. The most whimsical surface in the app.
- **A door.** The way out. When there's a reason to go — the flag is up, an invitation is waiting — it's where Mosey ends up, hopeful.
- **A mat.** Where Mosey sleeps and rests. Staying in is a full and honored choice, never a failure; the mat is its emblem.

**The Peek** is what you catch when you open the app: Mosey mid-something in this room. Interrogating a sock. Rearranging finds two inches to the left. At the window, watching the rain. Asleep on the mat in a shape that shouldn't be possible. The answer to "what is Mosey doing right now?" is the whole front door — never a menu, always a moment walked in on. Vignettes are time-, weather-, and history-aware: the morning Peek carries the forecast (Mosey's posture, §3.1) and last night's dream (§5.4); a flag-up morning finds Mosey at the door; a rainy morning is the window vigil.

**The homecoming.** Every walk ends back in this room. Mosey comes in, the walk gets recapped — a find shown off, the keepsake set in the window — and you can give Mosey a treat. Small, silly, entirely optional: no points, no counter, just Mosey's disproportionate joy over a biscuit. The treat is the period at the end of the walk's sentence, and the one reliably fun thing you *do* rather than watch.

**Hands on the world.** The app is mostly watched, but it can be touched — lightly, and that's the point. You can put a hat on Mosey (the bandana, the terrible raincoat, the one hat he has feelings about), place a found object on the Snail's shell or hand it to a resident, drop things in the mailbox to send, and arrange the shelf. Not a dress-up game; just enough tactile interaction to make the place yours. (The wardrobe is also the expression economy — §8.3.)

**The narrator reacts to what you do.** Touch isn't silent. Pet Mosey and the narrator has an opinion about it ("You pet Mosey. Mosey has decided you are good, actually."); give a treat, put on the hat, send a gift — each action can draw a dry, specific one-liner. This is a distinct content type (action → reaction), not a vignette that plays on its own: it fires *because you did something*, which makes the world feel responsive to your hand, not just observed. Rules as ever — dry, never gushing, rationed so it stays a small surprise rather than a reflex.

The Peek is the demo, the TikTok format, and the animation showcase in one surface. It is the first thing to prototype.

## 5.2 The mail system

**Incoming.** Six categories, each with a distinct job:

| Category | Job |
|---|---|
| Resident letters | Narrative drip; quest seeds with no quest log ("the Pigeon was last seen near the hedge") |
| Field reports | The week rendered as story, not stats; the being-seen reflections |
| Objects | Pressed flowers, stamps, seed packets, the unexplained key — slow-burn hooks |
| Invitations & postscripts | "P.S. — the Puddle is back" |
| Mosey's rare letters | The sincerity budget, delivered impossibly (postage: a leaf) |
| Nothing | Flag down. Essential — scarcity keeps the flag meaningful. Empty mailbox = Spider vignette |

Rules: mail never expires and never piles into counts; the flag is the only summons; letters wait patiently. No red badges, ever.

**Outgoing.** The mailbox is two-way. She can send a one-line reply or a found object, and she raises the flag herself — the send gesture is the "seal the envelope" moment. Sends are always answered: eventually, in kind, and disproportionately. The reply delay is a feature — every send schedules a future flag-up morning. She is, mechanically, mailing delight to her future self.

## 5.3 Invitations

The mechanic that turns a mailbox walk into more — designed so the decision to continue is made outside, where it's cheap (§1.3).

- The grammatical subject of every invitation is Mosey. Ears up at something down the street; a look; a lean. Mosey's curiosity, never her assignment.
- Frequency well under 100% — about half of mailbox trips. Predictable invitations become obligations; intermittent ones stay gifts.
- Chains cap at two links, rarely three, and every link pays off fully in place. Stopping anywhere is a whole story, not an abandoned one.
- Declining is frictionless and unrecorded: turn home and Mosey trots after her instantly — no disappointment animation, no comment, no data (doctrine 2).

The result: the app's only fixed ask is ninety seconds, and every longer walk is something she chose, outside, already in motion — self-authored, which is what builds "I'm someone who walks" better than any goal could.

## 5.4 Dreams

Overnight, Mosey dreams, and the dream is a surreal remix of her actual recent history: Tuesday's pinecone, enormous, ridden past the mailbox nemesis, who has grown legs. Available in the morning Peek.

Function: her own mundane life, returned as strange art. It claims the morning ritual slot (the territory of Wordle and weather apps), it's emotionally potent in a way stats can't be — Mosey dreams about *her* — and dream-logic licenses looser, cheaper, weirder animation. Dreams are also a callback vehicle: anything from the walk history can resurface here, transformed (doctrine 6).

## 5.5 The reflection layer

The "musings alongside walking" register — the app's quiet second job, delivered without one clinical word. Lives mostly at the Good Bench.

**The one-question walks.** Rarely, never as a survey, Mosey asks one oddly specific question — via the narrator: "Mosey would like to know what smell you miss." "Who taught you to whistle?" She answers in a sentence or skips (skips evaporate, per doctrine 2). The payoff is the callback, weeks later, unprompted: "You said you miss your grandmother's kitchen. Mosey thinks the street by the bakery might be adjacent to the topic. Mosey is not sure. Mosey has limited data." Being remembered is the deepest feel-seen mechanic available to software, and it's nearly free: stored strings plus callback slots.

**Dedications.** Before setting out, optionally: *this walk is for* — a person, a feeling, someone gone, her past self, "nobody, mind your business." Mosey carries it with appropriate gravity; the journal files it. People already process grief and big decisions on walks; no product has ever acknowledged that this is what walks are for. Handled with a light touch, this is the most emotionally serious feature in the app and the one users will tell each other about.

**Sit-with-me.** Not every session is a walk: a home mode where Mosey simply exists alongside her — reading, working, having a terrible day. No timer, no goal, no metric. Company without demand — the purest expression of the thesis, and the feature that keeps the app kind on the days she can't go out.

## 5.6 Finds and the gift economy

Walks yield finds — small, specific, narrator-annotated objects ("a stick of frankly Tortoise-grade quality"). What gives them life is circulation:

- **Sends ripple, never score.** Give the Snail the bottle cap: the Snail wears it permanently, in every future appearance. Send the Tortoise an acorn: three letters later it has "a place of honor." Objects gain biographies; the journal remembers which find became whose.
- **Two channels, two flavors:** the Mailbox send is a letter; the Snail-shell send is a voyage (weeks in transit, tracked by the narrator: "Day 9. The shipment has moved four meters. The shipment is thriving.").
- **Some finds are quietly marked as for someone** — a soft nudge that teaches the system and creates errands of affection without assigning one.
- **Soft requests.** A resident's letter mentions, in passing, a longing ("the Tortoise misses the smell of pine"). No marker, no timer — but pine-adjacent finds start appearing on tree-ring walks, and the payoff letter for sending one is disproportionate.

## 5.7 Collection is consequence, not completion

No completion meters — "14/20 Autumn Items" turns wonder into homework and homework into guilt. Instead, collecting changes the world:

- **Collections attract residents.** Enough shiny finds and word gets around: the Crow appears, interested, shameless. Enough round things and something starts nesting. Whatever she happens to collect determines who moves in over months — progression measured in new relationships, the only currency that fits this app. It also personalizes: the rain-walker's world fills with different residents than the golden-hour walker's.
- **Rarity comes from how she lived, not how much she ground.** Rare finds are gated behind world-states — rain-only, golden-hour-only, first-snow-only, fog-only. The rare thing flatters her life, not her grind or her wallet. Currency never touches find odds (§1.5).
- **Seasonal sets retire.** Autumn '27 never returns. Veterans get prestige; every season gets stakes; and a "missed" set is not a failure — her shelf tells the true story of her year, which is the point.
- **The shelf is the streak.** Accumulated, visible, unloseable history: the up-only number rendered as a place. Two users' homes look completely different after six months — through accumulation, not options.

## 5.8 Milestones

All milestones are up-only and unbreakable (doctrine 4): days together, the hundredth walk, gotcha day, the Sapling's growth marks, "one year since the weird pinecone." Milestones generate recurring emotional events with zero guilt mechanics, and they're screenshot-native. The largest ones draw on the sincerity budget (§3.3) — the anniversary letter is the canonical example.

## 5.9 Mosey's works

Mosey is writing something. The novel (word count: eleven — "Mosey called it a breakthrough"), three-line poems about wet leaves, unsent letters to the Squirrel. Occasionally she finds a scrap, with shared details woven in. Serial, unresolved, drip-fed: a plotline in a wellness app, and every scrap is a screenshot waiting to happen.

Production rule: authored templates with personal slots — never open-ended LLM generation. The voice is the asset; slop would poison it.

## 5.10 The Keepsake — the walk becomes a memory

This is the loop's payoff beat, and it does three jobs at once: it makes the memory, it fills the journal, and it is the app's primary organic growth engine. (It carries over the strongest single idea from the original pitch — the post-walk photo — reframed for Mosey's world.)

**The mechanic.** When a walk reaches somewhere — an invitation followed, a bench reached, a find worth marking — Mosey offers to *keep this*. Not every ninety-second mailbox trip; rarity keeps it precious. The camera opens, and **the illustrated Mosey composites into your real photo**, placed in the scene with a pose keyed to the walk: the weather, the light, the place, how far you went. Flat ink Mosey, sitting in your actual park, looking at something off-camera.

**Styled as a keepsake, not a selfie.** The result gets a cream border, a small date-and-place mark, and a **typed deadpan caption** from the narrator: *"You and Mosey, four minutes from home. Mosey is looking at something off-camera. There was nothing there."* It files into the journal as that walk's memory. It is never a performance prompt — no "share your streak," no camera nag. Quiet, personal, optional.

**The palette break is the point.** The real photo is full-color and shatters the ink-on-cream world — deliberately. The keepsake is the one place the real world enters the app: your messy, colored, actual life, with this small ink creature standing in it. The frame and the caption re-brand it. That contrast is the feeling.

**The dog-owner bridge.** Photograph your real dog and Mosey sits beside it in the frame — the acquisition channel for the entire dog-owner audience (§2.3), restored from the original's "photograph your real dog" unlock.

**Why it earns its place** (against the doctrines): the photo is the strongest possible journal artifact — a real image of a real moment beats a squiggle, and doctrine 6 (nothing is single-use; memory is meaning) is best served by real photos accumulating into the truest record of a year. It is "romanticize your life" (§2.2) made literal: noticing an ordinary moment and keeping it.

**Growth engine (ties to §7.7, §8.4).** Every shared keepsake carries an illustrated Mosey with a small watermark — organic acquisition, the animator's unfair advantage in the frame. It runs *alongside* the screen-recorded typewriter-moment channel: two share formats, the warm photo and the funny clip. Premium sells frames, seasonal overlays, and caption styles for the keepsake — the honest home for what was mislabeled "photo-overlay packs."

## 5.11 The journal — the memory spine

Every walk auto-generates an entry; the smallest counts. The journal is where the app's memory lives and where "the reward is never a number" is made concrete: no stats page, a keepsake shelf instead. It holds the walk (rendered as a non-geographic story-squiggle, never a route line — doctrine 8), the finds, the kept letters, and the keepsakes. It appreciates with time — six months in, it is the artifact a user would be upset to lose, and the thing they show a friend. It is also, quietly, the review-and-word-of-mouth engine: memory people made themselves is what they talk about.

# 6. FTUE — The First 48 Hours

## 6.1 The onboarding thesis

The data: Day-1 retention for iOS apps averages ~25%; 77% of users are gone within three days. Every screen before first value costs 10–15% of completions. Early permission requests trigger resistance. The wellness category's standard onboarding — a 10–20 question survey, a permission stack, a day-0 paywall — is optimized to harvest the motivated minority and burn everyone else.

Mosey's inversion: **ask for nothing.** No account, no survey, no goal-setting, no permission wall, no paywall, no notification prompt. For an audience that has been Noom-ed and Headspace-ed, being asked for nothing *is* the off-guard move — the first proof of "no bullshit" arrives as an absence. (The trend line agrees: apps that ask less are winning; Duolingo's highest-impact retention change was play-before-signup.)

The psychology throughout is Zeigarnik done ethically: open loops of **narrative** (what's in the mailbox? who lived here before?), never open loops of **task** (no progress bars, no "finish your setup," no incomplete anything). Curiosity without obligation — wonder can't be failed.

## 6.2 The honest opening — the problem, stated once

The fiction is the product, but the problem must be understood *before* the fiction starts, or Mosey reads as a toy. The fix is not explanation — it's recognition. The canonical four frames (§1.7) are the opening screens, verbatim:

> There's a whole world out there to explore.
> ...but some days you only make it to the mailbox.
> or maybe not even that.
> **Mosey. A walking companion. [pause] who understands <3**

Three beats, typed live, under fifteen seconds — and the user who has ever failed to leave the house has been *named* without being diagnosed. One plain-language screen follows, the app's entire self-explanation:

> No streaks. No counting. No guilt. [beat] Just one small walk at a time.

That is the last time the app explains itself. From here, the product demonstrates. (Marketing and onboarding are the same artifact — what the ad promised is literally what the first minute delivers. That identity is the no-bullshit positioning, operationalized.)

## 6.3 Day 1 — arrival (first session, ~4 minutes)

Start the story in the middle: the world must feel like it was already running before she arrived.

1. **Meet Mosey inside 30 seconds.** Name Mosey (or keep the name), one reactive vignette. No forms. *Mechanism: effort-free personalization — naming creates ownership before any ask.*
2. **One question, asked in-world, by the narrator:** "Mosey would like to know what kind of walks you two are." (strolls / errands / long rambles / "I barely go out, honestly"). One tap. *Mechanism: the entire personalization survey, compressed to a single in-fiction question — and the last option quietly tells the most vulnerable user she's expected here.*
3. **The first Peek:** Mosey at the window, staring at something. Outside: the mailbox. Flag up. *Mechanism: the core loop taught as an image, zero tutorial text.*
4. **The first mailbox walk.** The ask is calibrated to slipper-distance — for onboarding, "walk to your door or your mailbox with Mosey" — impossible to fail, complete in 90 seconds. *Mechanism: the tiny first win; competence before commitment.*
5. **Mail is already waiting:** the Squirrel's "NOTICE OF INTENT — THIRD NOTICE." Third — two prior notices she missed. And the Spider, mid-project, unbothered: "The spider was here before you. Respect the arrangement." *Mechanism: in-media-res depth. A world with a past implies a world with a future.*
6. **The map appears:** home, the mailbox, two faded smudges up the street. "There's more out there. Mosey knows what. Mosey's not telling." *Mechanism: curiosity gap, framed as a secret kept — not a checklist assigned.*
7. **Session close, on a plant:** Mosey at the window, watching the dark. "Mosey is expecting something. Mosey won't say what." *Mechanism: the open loop that carries her to tomorrow.*

**That night:** Mosey dreams — the first dream remixes her actual day (the notice, the spider, her). Waiting in the morning Peek. *A same-day reason to reopen, delivered as intimacy rather than a notification.*

## 6.4 Day 2 — the reveal

The flag is up. **Nobody on-screen raised it.**

Inside: the Tortoise's first letter — warm, unhurried, mid-journey — with the detonating line:

> *"I wrote to the previous resident for years. They never wrote back. I have a good feeling about you."*

Three payloads in one sentence: the world has a past (*who was the previous resident?* — a mystery arc that runs for a year), the mailbox goes both ways (the implicit ask — write back — teaches outgoing mail as an emotional act, not a feature), and a relationship has been offered. Enclosed: the seed packet. Plant it on today's walk — day 2 ends with the user having **put a future into the ground.** *Mechanism: endowment. She now owns something in this world that grows.*

Day 2's walk carries the first invitation (Mosey's ears; the blue fence; her choice, made outside) and yields the first find — "of frankly Tortoise-grade quality," per the narrator.

## 6.5 Day 3 — the loop closes

She has something to send and a flag to raise herself. The send teaches the gift economy; the reply, days later, teaches that this world answers. From here the FTUE is over and §5's systems carry the weight.

The 48-hour shape, compressed: **guaranteed payoff (day 1) → unexplained event + offered relationship + planted future (day 2) → first self-authored act (day 3).** Anticipation stacked on anticipation, entirely in-fiction, with no tutorial voice and nothing resembling a goal screen.

## 6.6 What we refuse to do — and what each refusal buys

| Refusal | What it buys |
|---|---|
| No account or email | The first session belongs to her, not the funnel |
| No survey wall | The one in-world question outperforms twenty form fields for felt personalization |
| No permission stack | Motion/location asked in-fiction at first real walk; notifications offered only after the first flag moment ("Want Mosey to mention when something arrives?") — each request arrives when its value is self-evident |
| No paywall in the first 48 hours | A deliberate trade: upfront paywalls convert 5–6x better on day 0, but companion apps monetize the relationship, and there is no relationship on day 0. Late converters retain better. The contextual paywall arrives days in, after the first warm moment (§8) |
| No notification prompt on open | The widget flag replaces push-begging entirely |
| No progress bar, no setup checklist | Nothing in the FTUE can be left incomplete, so nothing nags |

## 6.7 Comprehension checkpoints — the friend test

The fiction never explains itself, so comprehension must be verified, not assumed. Targets:

- **Minute 2:** she can say what the app is — "a little dog that gets you outside."
- **Day 2:** she can say why it's different — "it never guilts you; you just check the mailbox."
- **Week 1:** she can say it in the brand's own shape — "some days you only make it to the mailbox. That counts."

If playtesters can't pass the friend test at these marks, the fix is never more explanation — it's a sharper demonstration. The problem statement lives in the honest opening and the App Store listing; everywhere else, the product proves it.

# 7. Ongoing Experience & Content Engines

## 7.1 The freshness problem

The genre's decay curves are documented. Pokémon Sleep fell from $10.6M/month at peak to half that within eighteen months, despite Pokémon IP. Pikmin Bloom's growth is explicitly credited to events and live-ops — a team sport. AI companions churn 30% faster than non-AI apps because the character *is* the content, and characters exhaust. A solo builder cannot out-produce any of this with hands.

Mosey's structural answer: **the character is not the content — the world is, and reality is the live-ops calendar.** Weather, seasons, holidays, golden hour, first snow: an infinite, free, pre-installed event system. The job is not to generate novelty; it is to harvest it.

## 7.2 The five engines

Five independent systems, each rolling its own dice daily. The open answers "did anything happen in our world?" — and something almost always did, because five generators rarely all come up empty:

1. **Mail** — correspondence arcs, field reports, replies to sends arriving on their own schedule.
2. **Weather** — ephemeral residents (the Puddle), weather-gated finds, Mosey's forecast, weather vignettes. Rain is content that only exists today.
3. **Season & calendar** — the Tree's state machine, retiring find-sets, tenant turnover, migrations, anniversaries. Fires automatically off the date.
4. **Placement** — the Pigeon's misdeliveries: variable-distance walks and leaked lore, generated procedurally.
5. **Memory** — callbacks: dreams remixing her history, finds resurfacing, one-question answers returning, milestone dates. The engine that converts her own past into new content.

## 7.3 Authored spine, generated tissue

The rule that keeps generative content from feeling hollow (§5): feelings come from authored beats; freshness comes from generated tissue around them.

- **The spine is hand-written:** the Tortoise's seasonal arc (~12 letters), the sincere letters, resident arrival moments, the squirrel war's escalations. Small in volume, maximal in voice.
- **The tissue is templated:** narrator vignettes, forecasts, find annotations — templates with personal slots (weather, season, her walk history, her answers). The template is generic; the instantiation is hers, which is why it can't read as filler.
- **Never open-ended LLM generation.** The voice is the asset; slop poisons it. At most, a constrained variation layer inside authored templates.
- **Callbacks are infrastructure, not polish.** Every find, answer, and event carries a resurfacing chance — in a dream, a letter, a field report, an anniversary. Nothing is single-use (doctrine 6). This system does more for perceived depth per authoring hour than any other investment in the app.

## 7.4 The content calendar

Modeled on Finch's proven cadence (43 monthly seasonal events and counting, dual free/premium reward tracks, zero punishment), rebuilt for one author. Events are data, not code:

- **Monthly:** one find-set (5–8 items, retiring), one outfit capsule (the expression economy's supply line, §8), one community moment — a single day when every Mosey everywhere finds the same rare, weird thing. Shared inside joke, screenshot flood, lapsed users hear about it. Near-zero build cost after the first.
- **Quarterly:** one systemic beat — a new weather behavior, a resident arrival, a lore drop (a page of the previous resident's mail; what the key opens).
- **Annually, automatic:** seasons turn the Tree, sets retire, the Sapling grows, gotcha days and anniversaries fire per-user. Designed once, runs forever.
- **Blind-box psychology, ethically inverted (from Pop Mart):** surprise reveals, set completion, hidden rares, retired series — but odds are earned by walking in the world's states, never purchased. The dopamine stays; the exploitation doesn't.

## 7.5 Resident rollout is the update strategy

The cast ships in waves, and each arrival is an event the world predicted (§4's foreshadowing system — tracks, rumors, misdelivered mail — seeds every arrival 1–2 weeks ahead):

| When | Who | Why then |
|---|---|---|
| Launch | Mosey, Mailbox + Spider, Pigeon, Tree, Puddle, Bench | The complete core loop |
| Day 2 (per user) | The Tortoise (correspondence) | The long arc starts inside FTUE |
| Update one | The Snail | "Someone new has moved in" — re-engagement marketing as patch notes |
| Collection milestone (per user) | The Crow | Arrival is itself an achievement |
| Unannounced, seasonal | The Cat | Evidence first, appearance later; never explained |

New residents are the content-drop mechanism: cheaper than features, warmer than events, and each one adds a permanent engine (the Snail adds slow-courier sends; the Crow adds trades).

## 7.6 The comeback

The most-designed moment in the app (doctrine 4). She's been gone three weeks:

- Mail waited — never piled into a count. The flag is simply up.
- Mosey's reunion is joyful and disproportionate; the narrator records it without one word about the gap (there is no gap data to reference).
- The world caught up naturally: the Tree changed, a set retired, maybe the Cat came and went. Time passed *in* the world, not against her.
- The Cat is the lore-level guarantee: the world's most respected resident leaves for months and is welcomed back without explanation. Returning is normal here.

Two structural variants: **moving house** is a story arc, not a bug — Mosey re-scouts, a new map begins, anchors are re-auditioned ("the third bench was acceptable. Mosey is not ready to commit."). **Travel** gets the light version: camp, wherever you are, no re-basing.

## 7.7 The public character

Mosey has a life outside the app: the social account (TikTok-first) runs the character's ongoing lore — the squirrel war escalates, the Pigeon delivers something unforgivable, seasonal moods. Rules learned from Duolingo's playbook: the character is a persistent personality, not a mascot doing trends; the comments section writes the brief (the audience tells you which lore to canonize); and virality must route back to the core loop — every beat ends near the door, the mailbox, the walk.

Two share formats, both carrying the brand. The presentation layer is one: timed typewriter text over a Peek is natively screen-recorded vertical video with built-in comic timing. The keepsake (§5.10) is the other — a warm photo of a real walk with an illustrated Mosey in it, lower-effort to share than a video and more universal, and the growth mechanic the original pitch built around ("every photo carries the app"). Users make the video for laughs and the keepsake for love; both put an illustrated Mosey in front of strangers. Social content costs animation time only — no in-app development — which makes it the one channel where a solo animator outguns funded teams.

## 7.8 The authoring budget

The sustainable one-person monthly load, at steady state:

- 1 find-set (5–8 items: art + one narrator annotation each)
- 1 outfit capsule (2–4 pieces)
- 1 community-moment find
- ~12–15 new narrator lines into the vignette pool (the pool compounds; year two coasts on year one)
- 1–2 letters of spine maintenance (Tortoise arc, one-off correspondence)
- Social: 3–5 short character beats per week, drawn from the same animation library the app uses

Quarterly, one systemic beat replaces a month's find-set. Everything else — weather, seasons, callbacks, milestones, dreams — runs on systems built once. The test for any proposed content: *does this add to a pool or feed a treadmill?* Pools compound; treadmills eat you (the Zombies, Run! lesson). Only pool content gets made.

# 8. MVP & the Road There

## 8.1 What the MVP is

One sentence: **the Peek, the mailbox loop, the widget, and six residents, on iOS, in the full voice.** Everything else is staged.

**In scope:**

- The Peek (the app open; time/weather/history-aware vignette pool)
- The mailbox loop, both directions (flag, mail taxonomy, finds, sends, replies)
- Invitations (Mosey-embodied, two-link cap, declines unrecorded)
- The widget: flag state + weather-Mosey + one line. Non-negotiable — it is the notification system, the daily impression, and the weather-glance colonization in one surface
- The map artifact (self-filling, storybook), walk detection at slipper-distance calibration
- The text system complete: typed narrator with beats, afterthoughts, strikethroughs; handwritten letters
- FTUE per §6, including night-one dream (dreams ship simple: a small pool, the remix depth grows later)
- The Sapling (planted day 2)
- **The Keepsake** (§5.10): post-walk photo with illustrated Mosey composited in, cream frame, typed caption. This is the memory-maker and the primary growth engine — it earns MVP inclusion despite the AR-compositing lift. Ship simple: a few contextual poses, static placement, real-dog bridge can follow
- Launch cast: Mosey, Mailbox + Spider, Pigeon, Tree, Puddle, Bench; the Tortoise by correspondence from day 2; Squirrel by notices only
- Journal (walks as story-squiggles, finds, letters kept, keepsakes)

**Staged out (§7.5 rollout):** the Snail (update one), Crow (collection milestone), Cat (seasonal, unannounced), one-question walks and dedications (v1.x — the Bench ships as destination + sit first), sit-with-me mode, real-dog mode, Live Activity, Android, additional pen pals.

**The build order starts with the Peek + text system as a vertical slice** — it is simultaneously the hardest craft risk, the demo, and the social-content machine. If the slice doesn't make a stranger smile in ten seconds, nothing downstream matters; find out first.

## 8.2 The retention thesis, measured

The bet: companion-grade retention in a category with fitness-grade churn. Fitness apps average ~4% D30; companion apps run multiples of that; Finch holds ~60% D1. Working targets (hypotheses to calibrate in beta, not promises):

| Measure | Target | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| D1 retention | ≥ 45% | The FTUE's whole job |
| D30 retention | ≥ 15% | 4x the fitness baseline = thesis confirmed |
| Widget adoption | ≥ 40% of actives | The widget is the retention machine |
| Mailbox walks / user / week | ≥ 3 | The core behavior, not opens |
| Invitation acceptance | ~30–50% | Health of the momentum mechanic (measured in aggregate only) |
| First send by day 7 | ≥ 25% | The relationship turned reciprocal |
| Comeback rate (lapsed 14d+ who return within 60d) | Watch closely | The moment we designed hardest for |
| The friend test (§6.7) | Qualitative, every beta wave | Value comprehension |

Analytics constraint: measurement must honor the architecture — no route storage, no individual decline tracking (declines aren't data even for us), aggregate-only funnels. Privacy is a brand feature; the analytics stack is part of the brand.

## 8.3 Monetization: more of the world, not just cosmetics

The Finch model, adopted whole (§1.5): subscription + expression, monetized through love, never need. The pitch is more of the world, not a locked one — the subscription buys more of Mosey, not access to the dog.

- **Free, forever:** the entire core loop — walks, mail in and out, finds, sends, the widget, the journal, the residents, every sincere letter. The walk is never for sale; currency never touches find odds.
- **Premium unlocks more of the world:** more of Mosey being Mosey, more letters and weather and small strange happenings, the premium track of monthly sets (the Finch pattern: premium unlocks the set's centerpiece days earlier — gentle differential, no exclusion) — plus the expression layer on top: the wardrobe (bandanas, terrible raincoats, the one hat), home and journal cosmetics, keepsake frames and seasonal overlays and caption styles (§5.10), a supporter tag on Mosey's collar. It never guilts you, and only asks you to pay once you've fallen for the character.
- **Pricing:** $9.99/month, $59.99/year — about a coffee a month (working; the category skews annual). Launch accessible, hold pricing until retention is proven — the Finch playbook.
- **Paywall placement:** contextual, days in, after the first warm moment — never in the FTUE (§6.6). A quiet, always-open shop besides. We knowingly trade the day-0 spike for subscriber quality and brand trust.
- **Later, not now:** merch (a single-icon character earns a plush; the Jellycat demographic is literally §2.1), and gift subscriptions ("a walk buddy for someone having a year").
- **Revenue reality (from §2.5):** year one ~$45K quiet / ~$400K solid / $2.5M+ breakout; the difference between Aloe Bud's floor and Finch's ceiling is the daily loop plus this economy. The wardrobe pipeline is not polish — it is the P&L.

## 8.4 Go-to-market: the supporter path

Sequenced for a solo builder with craft as the only unfair advantage:

1. **The character goes public months before the app.** TikTok-first, 3–5 beats/week from the vertical slice's animation library (§7.7). Build the audience on Mosey; launch the app into it. Nothing else on this list works without this.
2. **Supporter beta (the WalkScape model):** paid early access via TestFlight — supporter tiers with cosmetic thank-yous (founder's collar tag), a waitlist, a Discord. Funds development ($2–10K/month changes solo survivability more than any post-launch lever), manufactures day-one reviews, and recruits the evangelists who will defend the app's tone in every comment section.
3. **Launch aligned to the January intent spike** — the fitness category's Super Bowl, survivable for us because the cohort churn that kills fitness apps in February is exactly what this design prevents.
4. **ASO:** draft on proven clusters — "self-care pet," "walking," "widget pet." Concede "step counter" and "pedometer" entirely. Screenshots lead with Mosey's face and one typed line, never UI.
5. **Apple featuring:** pursued seriously (crafted, wellbeing-positive, widget-forward — the editorial profile exactly), budgeted as a spike, never the plan.
6. **Japan/CJK localization** in year one (§2.6).
7. **Paid UA only after LTV is known,** and only Spark-Ads-style amplification of organically proven posts. On a shoestring, organic learns; paid scales what organic proved.

**Pre-launch checklist that cannot slip:** trademark search and handle registration for the name (both spellings, §1.2); the App Store listing carries the honest opening's problem statement; privacy copy states "no routes, ever" in plain language.

## 8.5 The risk list

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Fast follower (Finch ships "outside mode"; Widgetable adds a soul) | Speed to beta; the craft moat (reactive animation + comic timing don't bolt on); the supporter community as defensibility |
| Battery/privacy suspicion kills trust | Significant-motion APIs over continuous GPS; no-routes architecture stated plainly; the privacy stance as marketing |
| The gentleness tax (kind apps don't monetize) | The expression economy staffed as the business, not the polish — Aloe Bud is the tombstone of skipping this |
| Content treadmill | Pools, not treadmills (§7.8); five engines run on systems |
| Solo-capacity burnout | The authoring budget is the scope contract; anything over it is cut, not crunched |
| January cohort churns in February | That churn is fitness-app churn; the entire §5 architecture is the counter. Measure the January cohort separately |
| Read as a kids' app at first glance | The adult register (§3.7) is the whole defense: typography, palette, stillness, deadpan. Test first impressions on adults relentlessly |
| Featuring/virality never comes | The plan runs on the supporter path and retention; spikes are upside, not load-bearing |
| Apartment users / no mailbox culture | The Mailbox is Mosey's world-object on the illustrated map, not the user's literal mailbox — works on any block in any country; onboarding never assumes one |

## 8.6 Definition of done, v1

The MVP is ready to launch when: the vertical slice makes strangers smile without explanation; the friend test passes at all three marks (§6.7); a full user-week generates zero guilt-shaped moments in playtesting (audit every surface against the doctrines); the widget survives a home screen for two weeks of real use; and the 48-hour FTUE runs start to finish with no tutorial text. Not before.

# Appendix A — Locked Canon

*The single source of truth for decisions made to date. Migrates into sections as they're drafted.*

## The character *(migrated — §3.1 is authoritative)*

- **Mosey**: one dog, forever. No breeds, no variants (at most one adoption-time mark, e.g., a patch). Users rename him freely; wardrobe, learned quirks, and accumulated history are the personalization. "Customization through history, not configuration."
- Silent, not mute: full non-verbal sound design (sighs, huffs, tail thumps); never words.
- Completely sincere; never ironic. Has an inner life — projects, delusions, feuds (the sock interrogations, the novel, the squirrel war). Ratio: for every one thing Mosey does *for* you, Mosey is caught doing three inexplicable things for Mosey alone. Company, never a dependent — Mosey *wants* the walk, never *needs* it.
- Flaws are canon: afraid of one particular mailbox (not yours), vanity about finds, a nemesis squirrel never once caught, strong feelings about the vacuum.
- Philosophy rule: he may only philosophize about things he can smell, see, or eat. Profundity by accident.
- **Mosey is the weather report**: every morning Mosey has already checked; Mosey's posture is the forecast, captioned by the narrator. The widget = weather-reactive Mosey + one deadpan line.

## The narrator *(migrated — §3.2–3.3 are authoritative)*

- Unnamed, unexplained, never investigated. Dry, matter-of-fact, deadpan; the irony layer (the dog never is).
- **Reported speech** is the dialogue mechanism: "He says the shelf 'wasn't working.'" Translation reliability deliberately ambiguous.
- The user is never the butt of a joke. Declined invitations are never referenced (no data exists).
- **Sincerity economy**: narrator drops the deadpan 3–4 times/year max (letters, milestones). Rationed, therefore devastating.
- Long arc: narrator's register drifts from clinical toward fond over a user-year. Never acknowledged.
- No exclamation marks, anywhere.

## The text system *(migrated — §3.4 is authoritative)*

- **Narrator text = typewriter**: monospace (Courier Prime, working choice), character-timed reveal with *authored* beats (`[beat]` markup, ~800–1200ms; pauses at punctuation; hold before final clauses on comedy lines). The pause is the punchline's engine.
- **The typewriter is a dramatic medium** — it can't delete, only continue or strike. Two signature moves: **the afterthought** (statement, beat, an addition that reconsiders — the first thought stays on the record; ~1 in 4 lines) and **the strikethrough** (types, pauses, crosses out — strike stays visible — writes something simpler; ~1 in 15 or rarer; can interact with animation: narrator claims, Mosey visibly contradicts, narrator corrects the record). Both exist because they make the narrator vulnerable: thoughts evolve live, in front of the reader.
- **Mosey is always "Mosey"** in all text — never he, she, they, it, or "the dog." The repetition is part of the voice; renders as the user's chosen name in-app.
- **Anti-formula rule**: plain no-joke lines are the baseline (~half); shapes rotate; if five consecutive lines share a rhythm, break one. Punchline quality bar: true + unexpected angle, or end on the fact.
- **No cursor, ever** — blinking-cursor streaming is chatbot-coded (2026), the worst possible association.
- **Letters = handwriting, delivered whole** (a letter is an object, not a performance; read self-paced). Each correspondent has a hand: Tortoise slow loops; Squirrel furious small caps, underlines; Crow flamboyant; Mosey shaky and earnest (rare letters, leaf postage). The Pigeon doesn't write — it *stamps* ("DELIVERED WITH DISTINCTION").
- Tap always completes text instantly; reduced-motion honored; typewriter sound = soft low thock, mutable; **no bell on punchlines** (deadpan never signposts; the bell is diegetic mailbox sound only).
- Unifying fiction: **the whole app is a document being written** — the interface is the journal.
- Production flag: handwriting faces for CJK are hard; Japan matters commercially — plan stylized print-hand equivalents.

## Visual identity *(migrated — §3.7 is authoritative)*

- **Not kawaii.** Lineage: comic-strip modernist — Peanuts, Miffy/Bruna, Moomin; Gromit for silent acting; Wes Anderson-adjacent presentation (typewriter, symmetry, deadpan captions).
- Ink-on-cream. **Four values total**: warm paper cream, warm near-black ink, one signature accent (the collar/bandana + the mailbox flag red-orange), one ambient world-tone (golden hour amber, blue evening, gray drizzle). Mosey is constant; the world breathes.
- **The doodle test** is an acceptance criterion: a distracted person can draw Mosey on a napkin in ten seconds, recognizably. Fan-drawability is the distribution mechanism.
- The map: hand-drawn storybook artifact (Hundred Acre Wood endpapers), not-to-scale, no north, no blue dot; fills in as things are discovered.
- The Mailbox (accent-colored, flag up) is the second brand icon; candidate app-icon co-star.

## The world & mechanics *(migrated — §1 doctrines, §4, §5, §7 authoritative)*

- **Anchors born from behavior**: the app notices her real pauses/spots and proposes; she confirms or drags (relocation is one gesture, no questions — "He has agreed to relocate the operation. He asks no questions."). Distance rings auto-scale silently to the user's actual range (slipper-distance calibration; for some users the Mailbox is the front step — same world, same story).
- **Navigation = Mosey's nose**: bearing + distance rendered as acting (lean, ears, glances); generous arrival radius; no map UI on walks. Journal renders walks as non-geographic story-squiggles, never route lines.
- **Metrics doctrine**: never a future number; past-tense only, small, journal-only, never aggregated into comparison. One cumulative number exists — lifetime distance together — and the Tortoise holds it, reporting it in letters. Apple Health: quiet good citizen (write walks, read motion; never surface Health-style UI).
- **The Mailbox**: daily micro-loop; flag-up = the only summons (widget shows the flag; no push-guilt, no badges); mail never expires or piles into counts. **Two-way**: sends (letters back, found objects to residents) — user raises the flag herself for outgoing. Empty days are Spider days (see cast).
- **Invitations**: ~half of mailbox trips; embodied as Mosey's curiosity ("the grammatical subject is him"); chains cap at 2 (rarely 3) links, each complete in itself; declining is frictionless and unrecorded.
- **Mail taxonomy**: resident letters (narrative drip, quest seeds with no quest log) · narrator field reports (the week as story) · objects (pressed flowers, stamps, seed packets, the unexplained key) · invitations/postscripts · Mosey's rare sincere letters (arrive by mail, impossibly; postage is a leaf) · nothing (flag down; scarcity keeps the flag meaningful).
- **Finds & the gift economy**: sends are always answered — eventually, in kind, disproportionately (reply delay = scheduled future delight). Sends ripple (events, resident opinion, permanent costume changes), never score. Some finds are marked as clearly *for* someone ("a stick of frankly Tortoise-grade quality"). Collections attract residents (enough shiny things summon the Crow). Rarity comes from world-states lived (rain-only, golden-hour-only, first-snow-only), never volume or money. Retired seasonal sets; the shelf is the streak.
- **The Keepsake** (§5.10): post-walk photo, illustrated Mosey composited into the real image, cream frame + typed deadpan caption, files into the journal. Offered when a walk reaches somewhere, never every mailbox trip. The real-world palette break is intentional. Doubles as memory-maker and the primary organic growth engine (real-dog bridge included). Carried over from the original pitch's "post-run photo."
- **Home** (§5.1): one room, the same one, returned to after every walk. A window (weather + season + memory — Mosey faces away, looking out; old keepsakes can sit in the glass), a door (going out), a mat (rest — staying in is honored). The Peek is Mosey caught mid-something in this room.
- **The homecoming + treat** (§5.1): every walk ends home; recap the walk, set the keepsake in the window, give Mosey a treat — small, silly, optional, no counter. The one reliably fun thing you *do* rather than watch. (Carries over the original pitch's post-walk treat.)
- **Hands on the world** (§5.1): light, tactile interaction — dress Mosey (hat/bandana), place found objects on residents (the Snail's shell) or in the mailbox, arrange the shelf. Not a dress-up game; just enough touch to make it yours.
- **Action → reaction** (§5.1): the narrator reacts to what you do (pet Mosey, give a treat, put on the hat). A distinct content type that fires because you acted, not on its own. Dry, specific, rationed. Strikethrough rule: strike and stop (or replace inline), never restate the sentence minus the struck words.
- **Story structure**: vignettes and state, never linear seasons (the Zombies, Run! content-treadmill lesson). Authored spine (e.g., 12 Tortoise letters/season), generated tissue around it.

## The cast *(migrated — §4.5–4.7 are authoritative)*

*Two tiers: MEET (animated, present) and HEAR ABOUT (mentions, mail, evidence) — depth through hearsay. Foreshadowing is a formal system: every future resident seeded 1–2 weeks ahead, largely via Pigeon misdelivery. Naming rule: residents have no personal names — the narrator says "the [Animal]"; only Mosey is named. Landmark descriptors are few and plain; the honorific never spreads (no "good windowsill").*

- **The Mailbox + the Spider** — threshold device + the patron saint of empty days. The Spider predates you, never leaves, has rich ongoing projects, is thriving — the app's quiet blessing on staying home. Never explained.
- **The Pigeon** — earnest-dumb courier: maximum dedication, zero comprehension. Cannot read; addresses interpreted by vibes; every delivery a triumph ("delivered with distinction" — it has not been delivered). **Walks everywhere despite visible wings**, on an unarticulated principle. Head-tilt = entire cognitive process (animation showcase). With Mosey: strained professional courtesy; the incident is never discussed. **Misdeliveries are the world-exposition engine** — variable walks + other residents' mail leaking lore.
- **The Tortoise** — slow *with* direction; the long correspondence arc; holds the cumulative-distance number; **correspondence-only, possibly never seen**. First letter: day 2 (FTUE canon). Contains the previous-resident hook.
- **The Snail** — slow *without* direction, complete as it is; never ranked against the Tortoise (the two slownesses = the two valid walks). **The world's slowest courier**: shell-cargo sends, weeks in transit, always arrives; cargo-as-costume tints its identity. Trouble vignettes: low-stakes, never urgent, helping is mentioned never assigned. Patron saint of small progress.
- **The Crow** — charming villain; **trades, never steals** (loss aversion is the enemy's weapon, not ours): swaps your third-shiniest button for a suspicious "gift" and a note of magnificent self-regard ("AN UPGRADE. YOU'RE WELCOME. — C"). Never touches canon/sentimental items; everything recoverable, no deadlines; retrieval = negotiation comedy at its nest (a new ring). Arrives only after the shelf earns gossip ("your collection has attracted a criminal element. Congratulations.").
- **The Cat** — **serious, quietly sinister gravitas; regal, unaffected, shows no emotion; totally silent.** The narrator speculates about its thoughts, uneasily ("The Cat's tail moved once. The narrator has three theories. None are comforting."). Mosey respects it (reverence, some confusion — "Mosey greeted the Cat. The Cat received this."). Function: normalizes leaving-and-returning without apology (gone six weeks, offers no explanation, none was requested); the world's one purely anti-transactional resident; evidence-before-appearance (a shape on the fence at dusk).
- **The Squirrel** — the nemesis; escalating formal correspondence ("NOTICE OF INTENT — THIRD NOTICE"); sightings only in MVP; the war is eternal and no one remembers the original grievance.
- **The Tree** — the ancient one; the world's clock (seasons, first snow, blossom = live-ops for free); tenant system (residents take up temporary residency; Mosey has opinions about the vetting). Mosey respects it; the narrator stopped asking.
- **The Sapling** — the day-2 seed, planted where she chooses; grows in real weeks/months; the totem of the relationship; year three, something nests in it.
- **The Puddle** — ephemeral; exists only after rain; exclusive finds; converts the #1 walking excuse into the only day a location exists.
- **The Bench** — a destination (the point is you *went somewhere* to be still; home-sitting is home content); the walk that's really a sit, filed as complete; the reflection layer (one-question walks, dedications); recurring observed-never-met regulars (the jogger, the pigeon congress).
- **The previous resident** — the unseen mystery: unanswered letters, the unexplained key, things found that aren't yours. Fills the curiosity/long-arc need precisely by never appearing.

|---|---|---|
| Slow with direction ↔ slow without | Tortoise ↔ Snail | The two valid walks — never ranked |
| Fast & wrong ↔ slow & faithful | Pigeon ↔ Snail | The two couriers |
| Gives ↔ takes | Mosey ↔ Crow | Gift economy vs. trade racket |
| Never leaves ↔ returns unexplained | Spider ↔ Cat | Both fine. That's the point. |
| Earnest ↔ scheming | Mosey ↔ Squirrel | The eternal war |
| Emotional transparency ↔ total opacity | Mosey ↔ Cat | The narrator can read one of them |
| Present ↔ absent | Everyone ↔ the previous resident | The mystery under the world |

*The matrix encodes the thesis: almost every axis is a pair of equally legitimate ways to live. Nobody in this world is doing it wrong.*

## FTUE (locked beats — full treatment in §6)

- Day 1: meet & name Mosey (<30s, no forms) → Peek established (he's at the window; mailbox flag up) → first mailbox walk → mail already waiting (Squirrel's THIRD notice = pre-existing world; Spider in residence — "The spider was here before you. Respect the arrangement.") → map appears: home, mailbox, 2–3 faded smudges ("There's more out there. He knows what. He's not telling.") → close on anticipation ("He's expecting something. He won't say what."). That night: first dream (references the day).
- Day 2: **flag up, unraised by anyone on-screen** → the Tortoise's first letter: warm, mid-journey, and the detonator — *"I wrote to the previous resident for years. They never wrote back. I have a good feeling about you."* + seed packet (plant on today's walk) → first invitation → first find ("of frankly Tortoise-grade quality") → day 3 opens with something to send and a flag to raise herself.
- Principles: start mid-story; no tutorial voice; depth signaled as secrets kept, not markers assigned; every day ends with a planted tomorrow.

## Business & go-to-market (evidence in §2/§8)

- Monetize the relationship: subscription + expression economy (wardrobe, journal cosmetics, extra capacity). Never gate the walk, the dog's affection, the mail, or the core loop. Currency never touches find odds.
- Pricing reference: Finch-style ($9.99/mo, $59.99/yr band); H&F skews annual.
- iOS first, single platform. Japan/CJK localization early (genre over-indexes there).
- GTM: character-first TikTok months before launch (the timed-text presentation is natively video); supporter beta (WalkScape model) for funding + evangelists; Apple featuring pursued as a spike, never the plan; ASO drafts on "self-care pet" + "walk" clusters, concedes "step counter"/"pedometer."
